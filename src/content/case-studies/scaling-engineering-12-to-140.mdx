## The mandate

We had product-market fit and twelve engineers holding it together by knowing everything and everyone. The mandate was growth — but the real challenge was scaling the organization tenfold without losing the quality and judgment that twelve people in one room took for granted.

## What we changed

We invested in structure before we needed it: a leveled career framework, explicit team charters, and a hiring bar we refused to lower under pressure. We grew in deliberate waves, letting each new layer of leadership stabilize before adding the next.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The trap we avoided">
The temptation at every stage was to hire faster than we could onboard. Every time we resisted it, quality held; the one time we didn't, we spent two quarters recovering.
</Callout>

Managers were grown from within wherever possible, so culture propagated through people who already carried it.

## Outcomes

Engineering grew from 12 to 140 across six teams, regretted attrition stayed 42% below the industry benchmark, and the quality bar that defined the early team survived the scale.

## What I'd do again

I'd write the career framework earlier. We built it reactively, around month nine of rapid hiring, and spent months retrofitting expectations we could have set on day one.
