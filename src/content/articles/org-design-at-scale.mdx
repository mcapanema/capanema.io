The structures that work at twenty engineers quietly break at a hundred. Not loudly, not all at once — they erode, until one day the thing that used to take an afternoon takes a sprint and nobody can say why.

Scaling to 140 engineers taught me that org design is not about boxes and lines. It is about deciding where you want coordination to be cheap and where you are willing to let it be expensive.

## Optimize for the conversations you want

Every org structure makes some conversations easy and others hard. A team boundary is a bet that the people inside it will need to talk far more than the people across it. Get the bet right and work flows; get it wrong and you pay a coordination tax on everything.

So I stopped drawing org charts around technology and started drawing them around the conversations the business actually depended on.

<Pullquote>
You do not design an organization. You design the cost of its conversations, and the organization follows.
</Pullquote>

## Leave slack in the system

The other lesson was about slack. A team running at 100% utilization has no capacity to absorb the unexpected, and at scale the unexpected is constant. The teams that stayed healthy were the ones I deliberately under-loaded — and they consistently out-shipped the ones I packed full.

The decisions that held were few and unglamorous: clear ownership, real career levels, and the discipline to not hire faster than we could onboard. Everything clever I tried mattered less than getting those three boring things right.
