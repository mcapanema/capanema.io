"Build or buy?" is the question every engineering leader is asked, and it is almost always the wrong one. Framed that way, it collapses into a spreadsheet of license costs versus engineering hours — and spreadsheets have a way of hiding the only thing that matters.

The better question is: what do you want to be excellent at in three years?

## Excellence compounds where you invest

Everything you build, your team gets better at. Everything you buy, someone else's team gets better at. That is fine — you cannot be excellent at everything, and most of the stack is not where your advantage lives. Buy the undifferentiated heavy lifting without guilt.

But be honest about where your compounding advantage should sit, and refuse to outsource that, even when the spreadsheet says you should.

<Callout variant="info" title="The test I use">
If this capability were twice as good as anyone else's, would it change the business? If yes, build it. If no, buy it and move on.
</Callout>

## Buying is also a commitment

The trap in "buy" is treating it as the end of a decision rather than the start of a dependency. Every vendor is a bet on someone else's roadmap, pricing, and survival. Sometimes that bet is obviously right. The discipline is in noticing when you have quietly bought the one thing you most needed to own.
